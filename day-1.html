Section 1 — Opening Hook 0:00 – 0:05 · 5 min
Say This — Open With This Exact Energy
"Don't open with slides. Open with a question."
Walk in. Don't touch the laptop yet. Look around the room. Smile. Then ask:

"When you sent your last WhatsApp message two minutes ago — where did it actually go? Not 'to your friend.' I mean physically. Where did those words travel?"

Let them answer. Someone will say "internet." Someone might say "satellite." Accept everything. Then say:

"Let me show you something that will change how you see every app on your phone — forever."
  • Now open the laptop. Go to submarinecablemap.com. Project it. Zoom into India's coastline slowly.
  • Point at the cables: "See these orange and yellow lines under the ocean? Your WhatsApp message — and your ChatGPT reply — physically travelled through one of these."
  • Zoom out to show the full world map. Let the visual do the work for 10 seconds. Silence is fine here.
  • Then say: "95% of all international internet traffic goes through these cables. Not satellites. Cables. Laid on the ocean floor by ships."
💡 Energy tip: This opening works because it's visual and surprising. If you see jaws drop or people pull out their phones — perfect. That's the reaction you want.
🌊

There are over 400 active undersea cables stretching 1.3 million kilometres across every ocean on Earth. One cable cut near Mumbai in 2008 knocked out internet for 60 million users across South Asia.

Section 2 — How the Internet Actually Works 0:05 – 0:20 · 15 min
Explain Simply — No Jargon
Three Analogies That Make It Click

Say: "Before we talk about AI, let's understand the highway it drives on. The internet." Then use these three analogies one by one — pause and ask if it makes sense after each one.

Analogy 1
Internet = Postal System
  • Your message is a letter
  • Your phone is the post office
  • Packets = envelopes being routed
  • IP address = your home address
Analogy 2
Servers = Restaurants
  • You order (request a website)
  • Server cooks and prepares it
  • Dish delivered to your table (browser)
  • Bad connection = slow waiter
Analogy 3
Data Centers = Giant Libraries
  • All of YouTube lives in buildings
  • Size of 10 shopping malls, cooled 24/7
  • Google has 23 data centers globally
  • Mumbai and Chennai have major ones
Draw on Board
The Full Path
  • Your phone → Wi-Fi router
  • Router → Jio/Airtel ISP tower
  • ISP → undersea cable
  • Cable → OpenAI data center → AI
Step by Step — How a Website Loads
Walk Through This on the Board
1
You type google.com and press Enter
Like handing an address to a courier. Your browser needs to find where Google lives.
2
DNS lookup — finding the address
DNS is like a giant phone directory. It converts "google.com" into a number (IP address) that computers understand.
3
Request travels to Google's server
Your request goes through your router → ISP → possibly undersea cables → Google's nearest data center.
4
Server sends the webpage back
Google's server "cooks" your search results and sends them back as data packets, each taking a slightly different route.
5
Your browser assembles it into a page
All the packets arrive and your browser puts them together — like completing a puzzle — and shows you the result.
💡 Ask the class: "All of this happens in under 200 milliseconds. That is faster than a human blink. Does that make you see your phone differently?"
🔍

Google processes 8.5 billion searches every single day. That's 99,000 searches every second — including right now as you're sitting in this room. And each one completes that 5-step journey in under a second.

Section 3 — Journey of One ChatGPT Message 0:20 – 0:30 · 10 min
Make It Personal
Ask Everyone to Open ChatGPT Right Now

Say: "Everyone take out your phone or open a laptop. Go to chat.openai.com or the ChatGPT app. We're going to trace exactly what happens when you type a question."

Then draw this journey on the board as you explain — one step at a time, pointing at the board as you talk:

1. You type → 2. Encrypted & sent → 3. Travels to US data center → 4. GPU cluster processes it → 5. Answer sent back to you
  • Step 4 is the magic — explain: "The AI model is not a person. It's a mathematical system trained on billions of text examples. It predicts the best next word, then the next, then the next — until the answer is complete."
  • Say: "It doesn't 'know' anything the way you know something. It's pattern recognition at an unimaginable scale."
  • Ask: "Doesn't it feel different now — knowing your message physically travelled across an ocean floor and came back in 3 seconds?"
🖥️

ChatGPT runs on thousands of NVIDIA H100 GPUs in data centers. One H100 GPU costs around ₹25–30 lakh. OpenAI has tens of thousands of them running simultaneously. Every time you send a message, you're using infrastructure worth hundreds of crores — for free.

Section 4 — AI vs. Google Search 0:30 – 0:45 · 15 min
Live Demo — Do This on Projector
"Same question. Two tools. Completely different answers."
Open Google on one half of the screen, ChatGPT on the other. Type the exact same question into both simultaneously and let the class watch the results appear.
  • Query to use: "Plan a 3-day trip to Goa for 2 people under ₹10,000"
  • Google → sponsored ads, links to MakeMyTrip, travel blogs, booking sites. You still have to do all the work.
  • ChatGPT → complete day-by-day itinerary, budget breakdown, food spots, transport costs. Ready to use immediately.
  • Ask the class: "Which one actually helped you? Be honest." — nearly everyone will say ChatGPT.
  • Then land the key insight: "Google finds pages. AI reads them, thinks about your specific situation, and gives you the answer."
💡 Do a second query if time allows: "Explain compound interest like I'm 10 years old." Google gives textbook links. AI explains with a piggy bank story. The contrast is powerful.

Section 5 — What AI Is Good At vs. Bad At 0:45 – 1:00 · 15 min
Be Honest — Both Sides
AI Is Powerful. And AI Is Imperfect. Both Are True.

Say: "I want to give you a balanced picture. AI is not magic. It's an extremely powerful tool — and like every tool, it has things it does brilliantly, and things where it completely fails."

✅ AI Is Great At
  • Writing and improving drafts
  • Summarising long documents fast
  • Brainstorming ideas without judgment
  • Explaining complex topics simply
  • Language translation (100+ languages)
  • Writing and debugging code
  • Answering questions 24/7 patiently
⚠️ AI Struggles With
  • Getting specific facts right (hallucination)
  • Knowledge past its training date
  • Real-world judgment and common sense
  • Emotions, empathy, and real connection
  • Verifying if something is actually true
  • Knowing what it doesn't know
  • Local, regional, or niche knowledge
Critical Concept
What is AI Hallucination? (This One Really Matters)

Say: "Imagine you have a friend who is extremely confident. You ask them about a Bollywood movie you haven't seen — and instead of saying 'I haven't watched it', they make up an entire plot, characters, and even quote dialogue from it. Completely made up. Said with full confidence. That is exactly what AI does sometimes. It's called hallucination."

  • Live demo: Ask ChatGPT: "Tell me about the book 'The Blue Mango Tree' by Priya Sharma." — it likely doesn't exist. See what happens. Point out how confidently it may fabricate details.
  • The rule: Always verify important facts from AI using Google, official websites, or original sources. AI is your fast first draft — not your final source of truth.
  • Ask the class: "Has AI ever given you a wrong answer before? What did you do with that information?"
🎭

In a 2023 US court case, a lawyer submitted an AI-generated legal brief with 6 fake case citations — none of them existed. The judge fined him $5,000. The lesson: AI sounds authoritative even when it's completely wrong.

Section 8 — Hands-On Class Activity 1:30 – 2:00 · 30 min
Energy Check — Reset the Room
"Now we stop watching. We start doing."
"Everything you've heard in the last 90 minutes — forget it for now. The only thing that matters in the next 30 minutes is what you build with your own hands. Let's go."
  • Walk around the room as students work. Don't stand at the front.
  • Find the most hesitant-looking student first — sit next to them, look at their screen, say: "Just type something. Anything. There's no wrong answer."
  • For students who finish early: give them the bonus prompt immediately so there's no dead time.
Exercise 1 · 10 Minutes
The Goa Trip Planner

Give everyone this exact prompt to type into their AI tool:

"Plan a 3-day trip to Goa for 2 college friends with a total budget of ₹10,000. Include where to stay, what to eat, how to get there from [their city], and one must-see experience per day."
  • After 5 minutes: Ask: "Who got the most detailed plan? Read it out." — students love this.
  • Follow-up: "Now ask it to make it cheaper. Remove any hotel and use hostels only." — show how AI responds to follow-up instructions.
  • Discussion: "Whose trip plan looks most real? Whose looks like AI made it up? Why the difference?"
Exercise 2 · 10 Minutes
Write Your Professional Introduction

Now everyone uses the prompt they crafted during the instruction window — or a version of it — to write a personal introduction:

"Write a professional self-introduction for me. My name is [name], I study [course] at [college]. I'm interested in [career area or hobby]. Make it sound confident and warm — not robotic."
  • Ask 2–3 students to read theirs out loud. Point out what works and what sounds too AI-generated. The class will start to see the difference.
  • Then say: "Now tell the AI one thing about yourself that wasn't in the prompt — something personal — and ask it to include it." Watch how the output changes.
  • Key lesson to land: "The more you tell AI about yourself, the better it serves you. It only knows what you give it."
Bonus · If Time Allows
Ask AI Something You Actually Want to Know

Open the floor. Say: "Now ask it anything you've actually been curious about — about your career, your studies, anything. This one is just for you." Give 5 minutes of free exploration. Walk around and look at what students are asking — it tells you a lot about what they really care about.

💡 Common great questions students ask: career comparisons, "how do I learn [skill]", salary expectations, how to crack a specific interview — all perfect starting points for Day 2 discussions.
Section 9 — Closing Discussion 1:50 – 2:00 · 10 min
Don't Lecture — Just Ask
"Three questions. I want to hear what you actually think."
"I'm not going to give you answers here. These are questions worth thinking about — seriously. And there are no right or wrong responses."
  • Q1: "Will AI replace jobs — or create new ones? What do you think your work will look like in 10 years?" — let 2–3 people answer, build on their responses.
  • Q2: "What skills do you think will always be valuable, even when AI can do almost everything?" — push for specifics. Creativity? Judgment? Relationships?
  • Q3: "How should you use AI responsibly? What's one risk we haven't talked about yet?" — students often bring up cheating, fake news, job losses. All valid. Engage with each one.
  • Closing line: "The people who use AI well and still have strong human skills — judgment, empathy, creativity, real relationships — they will be unstoppable. That combination is what we're building here. See you tomorrow."

Delivery Tips — For Krunal
01
Move, Don't Stand

Walk around the room during explanations. Proximity creates attention. The front of the room is for demos only.

02
Gujarati for Warmth

Drop a Gujarati phrase when energy drops — "kem chho? samajyu?" It builds instant warmth and resets the room.

03
Pause After Big Lines

After "AI is your new English" — stop. Count to 5 silently. Let it sit. Rushing past big points kills impact.

04
Use "You" Not "Students"

"You can use this to…" not "students can use this." Personal language creates connection instantly.

05
Celebrate First Outputs

When a student gets a great AI response — celebrate it. "Show the class! This is exactly it." It motivates the whole room.

06
End Exactly on Time

2 hours, not 2:10. Ending on time builds trust. Students feel you respect them. That carries across all 30 days.
